←  200 BC
The oldest find of a money box dates from 200 BC Greek colony Priene, Asia Minor, and features the shape of a little Greek temple with a slit in the pediment. Money boxes of various forms were also excavated in Pompeii and Herculaneum, and appear quite frequently on late ancient provincial sites, particularly in Roman Britain and along the Rhine.

↑  1300–1400
By the 15th century, terracotta piggy banks were made in all shapes and sizes. Most of them were broken, having been excavated around the Majapahit capital of Wilwatikta (modern Trowulan). There are several examples on display at the National Museum of Indonesia in Jakarta, including this one to the right which was pieced back together from fragments by conservators.

←  1400
Intact ones are rare, of course, since you have to smash them to get your money, but here’s a 15th century one made out of terracotta in Oxford University’s Ashmolean Museum. If you have $5,000 in your piggy bank at home, take a hammer to it so you can buy this 15th century one in painted red clay. ↑  1920
In the 1920s, a new type of money box was launched by British banks, called the ‘home safe.’ The home safe was a little locked metal box given to customers, many of them children, to put their coins and banknotes in. The bank kept the key, so the customer had to take it to the bank to be unlocked. The money could put into bank account.
